## Narrative bible

### The setting
A fictional desert college town university called **Southwest A&M Tech** (SWATU), where marching band isn’t extracurricular—it’s *government.* The stadium is a coliseum. The practice field is a sacred grid. The band hall is an ancient labyrinth of haunted practice rooms and weaponized metronomes. The press box is literally “The Box,” where the gods watch (judges, directors, alumni, and… something else).

The band’s brand: **“The World’s Most Dangerous Marching Band (Allegedly).”**  
The joke: multiple schools across the nation claim to be “the world’s most dangerous,” which your story reinterprets as a cosmic copyright curse—reality can’t decide who the “world’s most dangerous” really is, so it fractures into timelines.

### Structure and themes
Your protagonist’s arc spans four academic years. The story is anime-melodramatic, but it lands emotionally because it’s about something real: going from “talented kid” to “artist” in an ensemble world.

Core themes you can repeat in dialogue, mechanics, and bosses:
- **Perfection vs musicianship:** a perfect dot with dead tone is still a loss.
- **Leadership vs rank:** first chair is not “main character license”—it’s responsibility.
- **Tradition vs evolution:** marching is discipline; jazz is listening; maturity is integrating both.
- **The ensemble as identity machine:** your “self” gets rebuilt by the group.

### Act breakdown (with JRPG escalation)
**Act I — Band Camp of the Damned**  
You arrive as a freshman trombone prodigy and instantly win first chair… which makes everyone hate you. You learn that “first chair” just means the spotlight is aimed at your mistakes. You meet the mascot—who acts like a clown, speaks in slogans, and seems to know the future.

**Act II — The Season and the Rivalry Engine**  
You travel, compete, and “recruit” party members (Pokémon structure) via auditions, transfers, and social quests. Each section is a clique with archetypes and ultimates. The mascot begins glitching reality. The Box judges begin speaking like JRPG antagonists.

**Act III — The Collapse**  
Injury, burnout, ego, and leadership conflict. The band fractures into factions (a literal faction system). Your perfectionism becomes a gameplay debuff. The mascot reveals it can force *Resets*—rewinding time to “run it again,” feeding on your desperation.

**Act IV — Improvisation**  
The forbidden Jazz Basement opens. You learn that jazz isn’t “breaking rules,” it’s “making promises and keeping them in time.” The finale is a living halftime show where the last “picture” morphs based on performance accuracy—your mechanics literally write the ending.

### Your mascot as a Kojima-style plot device
Mascot name (recommended): **PEP-0** (“Pep-Zero”), a sentient brand construct that looks like a ridiculous chili pepper in a shako hat.

Mascot progression:
- Early: comic relief, tutorial dispenser, fake merchandise hawker

## Gameplay systems and the game loop

### Overview: you have three interlocking modes
Your game becomes “one-shot-able” as a first project if you commit to **one vertical slice** that proves the loop.

**Mode A: Exploration (top-down / isometric-lite)**
- Walk around campus, dorms, practice fields, stadium tunnels.
- Talk to NPCs, trigger side quests (gear, social bonds, recruiting).
- “Encounters” are not random monsters; they’re *challenges*: auditions, sectional duels, rehearsal gauntlets.

**Mode B: Field Commander Mode (drill + formation tactics)**
This is the unique hook.

Core idea: drill is a sequence of formations, and those formations can be “pictures.” citeturn2search35  
Your game formalizes it:
- A “picture” = a target formation.
- A “set” = a time-stamped snapshot of positions (Set 1, Set 2, Set 3…), matching how real marching drill is documented in dot books/drill books. citeturn2search35turn2search5turn2search41
- A “move” = transition counts from one set to the next (8 counts, 16 counts, etc.). citeturn2search5turn2search41

Scoring pillars (simple, readable):
- **Cleanliness**: collisions avoided, spacing preserved
- **Alignment**: lines and arcs look “dressed”
- **Timing**: hit the set on the correct count
- **Energy**: how much crowd meter you built during the move

**Mode C: Spotlight Mode (rhythm performance)**
This is where your protagonist becomes “third-person” in the sense that it’s about *embodiment* (you are the trombone player performing).

The rhythm mode can be inspired by entity["video_game","Dance Dance Revolution","rhythm game series"] and entity["video_game","Guitar Hero","rhythm game series"], but your framing is “marching fundamentals and phrasing” rather than “notes on a highway.”  
Mechanically, you want:
- A stable BPM grid
- Input windows (Perfect/Great/Good/Miss)
- Combo + crowd meter
- Skill-trigger timing confirms

### The hybrid combat system (turn-based + rhythm confirms)
Turn-based gives you planning; rhythm gives you performance.

Flow:
1. Choose command (Skill / Item / Formation / Solo).
2. Execute a short rhythm confirm (easy early: 2–3 hits; later: polyrhythms).
3. Damage/buffs scale with accuracy.

Examples of command types:
- **Section Skills** (party): Brass Blast, Reed Hex, Battery Stun
- **Formation Skills** (Field Commander): Line Lock, Arc Mirror, Scatter Dodge
- **Solo Skills** (Spotlight): Slide Kick, Gliss Guard, Improvisation (late game)

Failure states are funny, not punishing:
- Missed confirm = the character “cracks” a note, loses a bit of morale, but the story treats it as cringe-comedy.

### The core game loop (day/week/season)
This is where your “Pokémon for marching band” fantasy becomes real:

**Daily loop**
- Morning: campus exploration + story choice
- Afternoon: rehearsal (Field Commander puzzle or fundamentals minigame)
- Evening: jam/duel (Spotlight rhythm challenge)
- Night: bond scene + allocate points (skill tree)

**Weekend loop**

- Competition = boss encounter
- Rewards: trophies, scholarships (currency), new drill formations, new music charts

### Rhythm timing technical note (why it matters)
Your rhythm system lives or dies on timing. In browsers, the Web Audio API’s clock (`AudioContext.currentTime`) is explicitly designed as an ever-increasing timestamp for scheduling playback. citeturn3search0  
Audio sources can be scheduled to start at an exact time using methods like `AudioBufferSourceNode.start(when)`. citeturn3search2  
If you use Tone.js, its Transport is designed to pass an accurate time into callbacks specifically because plain JS timing can be imprecise, and Web Audio scheduling provides sample-accurate timing for `start/stop/setValueAtTime`. citeturn3search20turn3search2

That’s your justification for building rhythm with an audio-clock “truth,” not frame timing.

### Field Commander Mode implementation (MVP approach)
Don’t simulate 100 individuals first. Simulate **sections as squads**:
- Each squad has a centroid + a formation shape (line/block/arc).
- A “set” defines squad target positions and orientations.
- “Cleanliness” scoring uses:
  - distance-to-target at beat end
  - overlap/collision checks between squad bounding boxes
  - alignment error (variance from line/arc)

Later, you can expand to individuals once the loop is fun.
